Living Life on Purpose

As a Christian, living life on purpose is deeply rooted in your faith and relationship with God. Here are some ways to live life on purpose as a Christian:

  1. Seek God's Will: Regularly seek God's guidance through prayer, meditation on His Word, and seeking wise counsel. Invite Him to reveal His purpose for your life and guide your decisions.
  2. Align with Biblical Principles: Base your values, goals, and actions on biblical principles. Let God's Word be your guide in every area of life, including relationships, work, finances, and decision-making.
  3. Serve Others: Follow Jesus' example of servant leadership by looking for opportunities to serve others with love and compassion. Use your gifts, talents, and resources to bless and uplift those around you.
  4. Share Your Faith: Live out your faith boldly and authentically, sharing the hope and love of Christ with others. Look for opportunities to share your testimony, invite others to church or Bible study, and engage in conversations about faith.
  5. Practice Forgiveness: Forgive others as Christ has forgiven you. Let go of bitterness, resentment, and anger, and extend grace and forgiveness to those who have wronged you.
  6. Grow Spiritually: Commit to growing spiritually through regular Bible study, worship, fellowship with other believers, and participation in spiritual disciplines like fasting and prayer. Seek to deepen your relationship with God and become more like Christ.
  7. Stewardship: Recognize that everything you have belongs to God, and practice good stewardship of your time, talents, finances, and resources. Use them wisely and for God's glory.
  8. Live with Hope: Anchor your life in the hope of eternal life with God. Let this hope motivate you to live faithfully, persevere through challenges, and trust in God's promises.

By living out your faith in these ways, you can experience a purposeful and fulfilling life as a Christian, bringing glory to God and blessing others along the way.

Discipleship According to Jesus: Lessons from Matthew 5-10

Discipleship is at the heart of Jesus' ministry, and nowhere is this more clearly demonstrated than in Matthew chapters 5 through 10. In these chapters, Jesus lays the foundation for what it means to be His follower, offering a blueprint that remains relevant for Christians today. From the Beatitudes to the sending of the twelve apostles, these passages provide profound insights into the nature of true discipleship.

The Beatitudes: A Portrait of the Disciple (Matthew 5:1-12)

Jesus begins His Sermon on the Mount with the Beatitudes, a series of blessings that describe the character of those who are truly His disciples. Each Beatitude reflects an aspect of the disciple's heart:

  • Poor in Spirit: Recognizing our spiritual need and dependence on God.
  • Mourning: Feeling the weight of sin and the brokenness of the world.
  • Meekness: Exercising humility and gentleness, trusting in God's justice.
  • Hunger and Thirst for Righteousness: A deep desire to live in accordance with God's will.
  • Merciful: Showing compassion to others as God has shown to us.
  • Pure in Heart: Living with integrity, motivated by a sincere love for God.
  • Peacemakers: Working to reconcile others to God and each other.
  • Persecuted for Righteousness: Willingness to suffer for the sake of Christ.

These qualities are not optional; they are essential to the life of a disciple. Jesus teaches that those who embody these traits will experience God's kingdom, comfort, and blessing, even in the face of opposition.

Salt and Light: The Influence of the Disciple (Matthew 5:13-16)

After describing the character of His followers, Jesus turns to their role in the world. Disciples are called to be "salt" and "light"—preserving moral goodness and illuminating truth in a world darkened by sin. This passage underscores the importance of living out one's faith publicly, allowing others to see the transformative power of Christ in action. The disciple's life should provoke curiosity and point others toward God.

The Fulfillment of the Law: Righteousness from the Heart (Matthew 5:17-48)

In this section, Jesus addresses the relationship between discipleship and the Law. He clarifies that He has not come to abolish the Law but to fulfill it, raising the standard of righteousness. True discipleship goes beyond mere external obedience; it requires a transformed heart. Jesus teaches that anger is akin to murder, lust to adultery, and that love must extend even to one's enemies. These radical teachings challenge disciples to reflect God's perfect love and justice in their daily lives.

Practical Discipleship: Prayer, Fasting, and Trust (Matthew 6)

In chapter 6, Jesus shifts from the attitudes of the heart to the practices of the disciple. He teaches about the importance of sincerity in spiritual disciplines like giving, prayer, and fasting. These practices are not meant to be performed for human approval but as acts of devotion to God. Jesus also instructs His followers on the Lord’s Prayer, emphasizing the need for daily dependence on God, forgiveness, and spiritual vigilance.

Moreover, Jesus warns against the dangers of materialism, teaching that true disciples do not store up treasures on earth but in heaven. He calls for radical trust in God's provision, encouraging His followers to seek first His kingdom and righteousness.

Judging, Asking, and the Narrow Way (Matthew 7)

Chapter 7 continues with practical instructions on how disciples should live in relation to others. Jesus warns against hypocritical judgment, instructing His followers to first examine their own lives before addressing the faults of others. He also encourages persistence in prayer, assuring that God will give good gifts to those who ask.

Jesus then presents the stark reality of discipleship: the path is narrow, and few find it. True discipleship requires intentionality and commitment, as opposed to the wide, easy road that leads to destruction. This passage serves as a sobering reminder that following Jesus is a call to a countercultural way of life.

Discipleship in Action: Jesus Illustrates His Teachings (Matthew 8-9)

Chapters 8 and 9 of Matthew serve as a powerful illustration of the teachings Jesus delivered in chapters 5 through 7. These chapters are filled with examples of how Jesus lived out the principles He had taught, demonstrating His authority and compassion in action.

  • Healing the Sick: In these chapters, Jesus heals a leper, a centurion's servant, Peter's mother-in-law, and many others who were sick or demon-possessed (Matthew 8:1-17). These acts of healing show His compassion for the suffering and His power over physical and spiritual ailments. Jesus' willingness to touch the untouchable and respond to the faith of a Roman centurion exemplifies the love and mercy He taught in the Beatitudes.
  • Calming the Storm: When Jesus calms the storm on the Sea of Galilee (Matthew 8:23-27), He demonstrates His authority over nature, reinforcing the lesson of trust in God's provision and protection from Matthew 6. The disciples' fear and Jesus' rebuke highlight the importance of faith and the assurance that God is in control, even in life's most turbulent moments.
  • Casting Out Demons: Jesus' encounters with demonic forces (Matthew 8:28-34) show His authority over evil, a theme central to the disciple's role as a light in a dark world. By driving out demons, Jesus illustrates the victory over sin and darkness that His followers are called to participate in.
  • Forgiving Sins and Healing the Paralytic: In Matthew 9:1-8, Jesus not only heals a paralyzed man but also forgives his sins, demonstrating His authority to address both physical and spiritual needs. This episode emphasizes the comprehensive nature of Jesus' mission and the holistic approach to discipleship that includes both physical and spiritual restoration.
  • Calling Matthew and Associating with Sinners: When Jesus calls Matthew, a tax collector, to follow Him and dines with sinners (Matthew 9:9-13), He exemplifies the inclusive love and grace He preached. This act underscores the message that true discipleship is not about self-righteousness but about mercy, compassion, and a willingness to reach out to those society deems unworthy.
  • Restoring Life and Healing: Jesus' miracles of raising Jairus' daughter and healing the woman with the issue of blood (Matthew 9:18-26) further demonstrate His power over life and death. These miracles reinforce the teachings of hope and faith, showing that nothing is impossible for God.
  • Sending Out the Twelve: At the end of chapter 9, Jesus sends out His disciples to preach the gospel, heal the sick, and cast out demons (Matthew 9:35-10:1). This commissioning illustrates the transition from learning to doing, as the disciples are now called to put into practice all they have learned from Jesus.

The Cost of Discipleship: Sending Out the Twelve (Matthew 10)

In chapter 10, Jesus prepares His twelve disciples for the mission, giving them authority to heal and cast out demons. However, He also warns them of the challenges they will face. Discipleship, according to Jesus, comes with a cost. His followers will face persecution, rejection, and even betrayal by loved ones. Yet, Jesus assures them of God's care and the eternal rewards awaiting those who endure.

Jesus also emphasizes the need for complete allegiance to Him, stating that anyone who loves family more than Him is not worthy of Him. This radical call to loyalty underscores the seriousness of discipleship.

Conclusion: The Call to Discipleship

Matthew 5-10 offers a comprehensive picture of what it means to be a disciple of Jesus. It is a life marked by humility, righteousness, and a deep commitment to God's kingdom. Discipleship is not merely about following religious rules but a profound transformation of heart and life.

Chapters 8 and 9 demonstrate that Jesus taught about discipleship and lived it out in every aspect of His ministry. His actions provide a model for us to follow as we seek to live out the principles He has taught. Discipleship requires us to listen to Jesus' words and actively participate in His mission, bringing healing, hope, and the gospel to a world in need.

As we reflect on these teachings, we are challenged to examine our walk with Christ. Are we embodying the Beatitudes? Are we being salt and light in our communities? Are we willing to count the cost and follow Jesus wherever He leads? True discipleship requires nothing less than our whole lives, surrendered to the One who gave His life for us.

Selfish Deeds

Galatians 5:19-21 provides a detailed list of behaviours and attitudes that are contrary to the Christian life. These include:

  1. Sexual Immorality: Any form of sexual activity outside the bounds of marriage between a man and a woman.
  2. Impurity: Moral uncleanness or actions that defile one's character or integrity.
  3. Lustful Pleasures: Pursuing selfish desires and indulging in pleasures that are contrary to God's will.
  4. Idolatry: Worshiping anything or anyone other than God, placing created things above the Creator.
  5. Sorcery: Involvement in occult practices or seeking spiritual power outside of God's authority.
  6. Hatred: Deep-seated animosity and hostility towards others.
  7. Quarreling: Engaging in heated arguments and disputes that lead to division and discord.
  8. Jealousy: Envious resentment towards others' success, possessions, or blessings.
  9. Outbursts of Anger: Uncontrolled rage and explosive reactions to situations or people.
  10. Selfish Ambition: Pursuing personal gain and status at the expense of others.
  11. Dissension: Causing division, strife, and conflict within the Christian community.
  12. Factions: Forming cliques or groups that promote division and exclusivity.
  13. Envy: Coveting what others have and desiring to possess it for oneself.
  14. Drunkenness: Excessive consumption of alcohol leading to loss of self-control and judgment.
  15. Orgies: Participation in wild and unrestrained parties or gatherings that promote sinful behaviour.

The passage concludes by warning that those who practice such things will not inherit the kingdom of God.

The Fruit of the Spirit

Galatians 5:22-23 highlights the fruits of the Spirit, which are the desirable qualities and characteristics that should be evident in the life of a Christian:

  1. Love: Agape love, is characterized by selflessness, compassion, and genuine concern for others.
  2. Joy: A deep-seated gladness and contentment that comes from knowing God and His promises.
  3. Peace: Inner tranquillity and harmony, regardless of external circumstances, rooted in trust in God.
  4. Patience: Endurance and forbearance in difficult situations, showing restraint and tolerance towards others.
  5. Kindness: Acts of goodness, generosity, and benevolence towards others, reflecting God's loving nature.
  6. Goodness: Moral excellence and integrity, doing what is right and just in all situations.
  7. Faithfulness: Loyalty, reliability, and trustworthiness in keeping promises and commitments.
  8. Gentleness: Humility, meekness, and considerate behaviour towards others, avoiding harshness or aggression.
  9. Self-Control: Mastery over one's desires, impulses, and actions, exercising discipline and moderation.

These fruits of the Spirit are evidence of a transformed life guided by the Holy Spirit. They enable believers to reflect the character of Christ and bear witness to His love and grace in the world.

True Nature of Discipleship

In Matthew 7:21-23, Jesus delivers a sobering message about the true nature of discipleship. He emphasizes that mere verbal acknowledgment of Him as Lord is insufficient for entering the kingdom of heaven. Instead, genuine discipleship is marked by obedience to the will of God. This passage underscores the importance of a sincere, transformative relationship with God, one that reflects His values and aligns with His purposes. It warns against relying solely on external actions or religious practices, stressing the necessity of authentic faith expressed through obedience and a heart surrendered to God.

Furthermore, Jesus highlights the danger of self-deception within religious contexts. He portrays a scenario where individuals claim to have performed miraculous deeds and spiritual acts in His name. Despite their outward displays of religious fervor, Jesus reveals that their hearts were not aligned with God's will. This serves as a cautionary tale, urging believers to examine the authenticity of their faith and ensure that it is rooted in a genuine relationship with God rather than superficial acts or religious rituals. The passage challenges us to prioritize a deep, intimate connection with God and live out our faith in obedience and sincerity.
